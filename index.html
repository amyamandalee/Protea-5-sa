The genus Protea was named by Carl Linnaeus in 1735, possibly after the Greek god Proteus, who was able to change its shape at will, possibly because they have such a wide variety of forms. Linnaeus 'genus was formed by merging a number of genera previously published by Herman Boerhaave, although exactly which of Boerhaave's genera in Linnaeus' Protea varied with each publication of Linnaeus.

The family Proteaceae, to which Protea species belong, is an ancient one among angiosperms. Evidence from pollen fossils suggests that Proteaceae ancestors grew in Gondwana in the Upper Cretaceous 75-80 million years ago.[2] The Proteaceae are divided into two subfamilies: the Proteoideae, which are best represented in southern Africa, and the Grevilleoideae, which are concentrated in Australia and South America, and the other smaller segments of Gondwana, which are now part of East Asia.
